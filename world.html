  5. Like most teenagers, I spent a lot of my free time on my computer. There was a particular website I was fairly active on called Tumblr, a blogging platform where I engaged in
  6. what felt like another culture-—sharing “memes,” bonding with other users based on similar music/pop culture interests, and learning about being socially conscious. I owe a
  7. good amount of who I am today based on Tumblr, specifically my humor, liberal beliefs, and interestingly enough, my professional career.
  9. As mentioned earlier, Tumblr is a blogging platform, suggesting that a user has the ability to create a blog using either premade templates or using HTML/CSS script code to
  10. design their own. Tending to my curiosity one day, I selected the option to create the blog page manually with code, knowing absolutely nothing about what code even was. What I
  11. was immediately met with was a variety of abstract characters, composed together to create words that had no meaning to me, but translated beautifully into images and visual
  12. structures on my laptop screen. I was astonished by the way these nonsensical keywords were able to produce entire web pages and run the entire Tumblr platform. I recall
  13. playing around with different keywords, immediately learning how to change the background color of my blog page and feeling excited. I thought highly of users who had
  14. beautiful blog pages and recognized that code held so much power. Not only did I recognize it as a tool to build web pages, but I considered it as a tool for creativity,
  15. creating visually stimulating pages and channeling one’s artistic energy.
  17. Up until this point of my life, I never considered myself creative. I didn’t have any desire to engage in artistic activities as the schools I attended never pushed
  18. for the arts. Learning how to code and advancing my knowledge of web development was an artistic outlet I created for myself, excited to learn new things. Although
  19. coding is an activity that requires analytical and critical thinking, I especially consider it to be a creative pursuit as it calls for using your ideas to solve problems
  20. and design pages that are beautiful to experience. According to Paul Willis and his piece “Symbolic Creativity,” those in late developmental stages, such as
  21. teenagers and young adults, are susceptible to finding and cultivating their own creativity in order to find cultural significance in their day to day life.
  22. Specifically, he shares that “[they] seek to creatively establish their presence, identity, and meaning through [signs and symbols] of everyday life," (Willis, 2).
  24. Applying creativity to daily life and the activities one participates in is necessary to cultivate the skill of problem solving and a solution-driven mindset,
  25. making creative thinking a requirement for coding. I’ve carried this way of thinking throughout studying computer science in my undergraduate career and it has
  26. helped me in becoming technologically literate and tapping into my problem thinking skills, both academically and in my personal life.
